How I do it

There's a lot involved in making a great product.

This is what goes into one.

User-focused design

I belive that design can genuinely make or break a product, but it's often further down the product backlog than it should be.

I treat design like a commodity; it's every user's first impression, and unlike that cliche we love to throw around, we actually do judge books by their covers.

Design isn't just aesthetics though, it's as much about how the product is laid out and intended to be used. I strongly believe in designing easy-to-to-use products that have a clean, smooth look.

Responsive websites

The only thing worse than egg salad that's been left in the sun for slightly too long is a website that loads slower than Southern molasses, and clearly hasn't been optimised for mobile.

Mobile devices are now royalty where browsing is concerned; heavy pages and haphazard layouts really don't cater to a website's first class users.

Bootstrap or otherwise, my products are built mobile-first (where applicable), and are intended to be as light as is reasonable.

Fast, secure backends

I want my backend code to be three things: fast, and safe, and well designed. That last one affects developers more than anyone else, but from the end user's perspective the first two attributes take precedence.

Any product I build that has any form of backend interaction has to be served over HTTPS and have proper security measures built into it; from properly hashed passwords to cross-site-scripting protection.

Server side code (API or otherwise) should execute quickly and be designed to scale. Nobody really cares if you're in peak hours, they just want their delivery confirmation dammit.

DevOps know-how

Building a great product isn't enough, you also have to ship and maintain those products. I take the DevOps approach to solving both these problems.

I think unit tests save lives (if not lives then sanity, user impressions, and work relationships), scripting is one of a software engineer's most valuable tools, Continuous Delivery is a necessity, and servers shouldn't be touched manually.

Taking a little bit of time to properly implement the above means I don't produce codebases held togther by hopes and prayers, and also means I can deploy to production regularly, with confidence.
